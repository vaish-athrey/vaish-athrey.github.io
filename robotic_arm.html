Visual impairment occurs when an individual loses vision due to issues in the eyes or brain, and the World Health Organization defines blindness as having a corrected visual acuity in the better eye of less than 3/60. It disproportionately affects children in low-income regions, particularly in Africa and Asia, where education can be their only way to improve their circumstances. Historically, visually impaired children have faced significant barriers to education, largely due to socio-economic factors.
The development of the Braille alphabet by Louis Braille in 1824 revolutionized education for the visually impaired, enabling them to read through a system of tactile dots. The Braille system, comprising six dots arranged in a grid, allows for 64 unique configurations and is used to encode most modern languages. However, traditional braille textbooks face challenges, including wear and tear of embossed alphabets, limited production due to reliance on manual typewriters, and a lack of widespread publishers.
To address these issues, an automated device is proposed that can convert speech and text into braille script, offering significant benefits to visually impaired students. The device would store large amounts of data, provide real-time conversion into braille, and include audio output to further support learning.  The methodology for the voice-controlled robotic arm involves integrating speech recognition with robotic arm control systems to enable hands-free operation. First, audio input is captured through a microphone, which is interfaced with a microcontroller like the Raspberry Pi. The captured voice commands are processed using a speech recognition library, such as Google’s Web Speech API, which converts the spoken commands into text. This text is then parsed to identify specific actions, such as moving the robotic arm in different directions (e.g., "move left," "move right"), or performing tasks like picking up objects or rotating the arm. Once the command is identified, the microcontroller processes the instructions and sends signals to the arm's motors or servos to carry out the desired movement. Additionally, the robotic arm may incorporate feedback mechanisms like sensors to confirm the arm's position and ensure accuracy in execution. Visual or auditory feedback is provided to the user through an LCD display or a speaker to confirm the action has been successfully carried out. This feedback loop helps in ensuring the robotic arm operates effectively in response to voice commands and provides real-time confirmation of actions. The system is designed to allow for efficient, hands-free control of the robotic arm for a variety of tasks.
